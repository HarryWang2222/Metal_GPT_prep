Abstract: The authors agree with Wilkens' results and with his new derivation showing that the average interaction energy between many dislocations distributed in an uncorrelated and quasi-uniform manner over the cross-section of a finite body is zero. This result implies that the interaction energy between just two dislocations in a finite body does not have a unique sign. To illustrate this point, the problem of two screw dislocationa parallel to the axis of a long circular cylinder has been solved explicitly. Abstract: The effusion of hydrogen from deformed iron wires has been measured by combining low temperature internal friction measurements of the cold-work peak with a thermal cycling procedure. Diffusion constants have been calculated by a modification of the technique developed by Armstrong, for conditions where diffusion takes place over a range of temperatures at a constant hsating rate. The activation energy for effusion between 200 and 300°K is 7–8 kcal/g-mole, in good agreement with previous work based on other methods. A linear variation of the cold-work peak with the percentage of hydrogen suggests that dislocations act as the major trapping sites when the deformation level (16 per cent R.A.) is insufficient to produce internal voids, and when the hydrogen content is low (~1 ppm). A binding energy of 4–6 kcal/g-mole can be derived by taking into account current values for the basic lattice diffusion of hydrogen. This is consistent with a basic elastic interaction energy model based on a partial molar volume for hydrogen of~2 cc/g-mole, and supports the location of lattice hydrogen in octahedral interstices. The similarity between the dislocation binding energy, and the energy involved in adsorption at free surfaces, may account for the conflict which exists regarding the predominant location of hydrogen in alloys containing both internal voids and a high dislocation density.

Abstract: Incubation times and frequency factors are derived for some cases of heterogeneous nucleation on grain boundaries. The incubation time in all cases is found to be proportional to the critical nucleus volume squared divided by the frequency factor and free energy of critical nucleus formation. It is shown that an energetically favorable reaction possibly may not occur if another reaction with a smaller incubation time is possible. General methods for calculation of frequency factors and incubation times are also given.

Abstract: The creep behavior of a quenched and tempered Fe-Mo-C steel was investigated over the range of the magnetic Curie transformation from 875° to 1075°K. The apparent activation energy for creep was insensitive to the applied stress and had the same value over the primary and secondary stages. It exhibited the same type of hump at the Curie transformation temperature as that observed for the apparent activation energy for creep of α-Fe, which it exceeded by about 11,000 cal/mole. The secondary creep rate of this alloy increased with the 6.8 power of the applied stress in complete agreement with similar trends for α-Fe. The secondary creep rate, however, was about 10−3 that of α-Fe due principally to its higher activation energy for creep.

Abstract: A 5 MHz ultrasonic transducer transmission method was used to study recovery processes subsequent to plastic deformation in a polycrystalline, high purity iron-0.05% carbon alloy. Simultaneous and continuous measurements were made of stress relaxation and attenuation recovery in the temperature range 77°K < T < 300°K. Below about 180°K, the effect of interstitial pinning of dislocations was Found to be negligible. Above this temperature, interstitial pinning was found to be a two-stage thermally activated process with a time exponent of 23 in the first stage, changing to 12 in the second. The activation energy of the first stage was determined as 12.5 kcal/mole by a Granato-Hikata-Lücke analysis and as 11 kcal/mole by an Arrhenius analysis of the time to transition from the first to the second stage. It is argued that for the first stage these values are consistent with Schoeck and Seeger re-organization of carbon interstitials about strain induced screw dislocations. The second stage is believed to be Cottrell-Bilby bulk diffusion toward the dislocations.

Over a limited temperature range, a thennochemical model was found to agree reasonably well with the measured phase boundaries at high pressure. Chromium was found to have a profoundly different influence on structure than that observed, for example, Ni in Fe-Ni alloys in that alloys quenched from the gamma loop at 40 kb exhibit structures of types which have not been observed previously. Abstract: The lengths of lines in the (225)F martensite habit plane of an Fe-Cr-C alloy have been compared with the corresponding lengths in austenite before transformation, by the use of dimensionally stable replicas of the untransformed austenite. In particular no significant changes in length were observed for lines parallel to [11̄0]F even though the transformation of [11̄0]F into [111̄]B involves a reduction in interatomic distance of 1.8%. Abstract: Aluminium products containing a three-dimensional network of closely spaced oxide particles have been studied by transmission electron microscopy and tensile testing at room temperature and 400°C. It is shown that the relationship between the tensile stress (0.01% and 0.2% offset) and the mesh size tm of the oxide network in a plane can be expressed by the equation σ = σ0 + k · tm−12 where k is a constant independant of the mesh size. Abstract: The fracture stress at the ductile-brittle transition temperature of polycrystalline molybdenum was found to be constant, 64± 2 kg mm−2, as the strain rate was raised from 5 × 10−6to 5 × 10−2sec−1. It is suggested that only above the transition temperature is crack nucleation easier than crack propagation and that brittle fracture takes place at the crack nucleation stress. At the transition temperature the stresses for yielding, crack nucleation and crack propagation are thought to be equal. Assuming simple phenomenological correlations between yield stress and temperature, strain rate and grain size, respectively, an analysis of the dependence of the ductile-brittle transition temperature on strain rate, neutron irradiation, pressurization and grain size is presented. This analysis should be applicable to the other Group Via b.c.c. transition metals: chromium and tungsten, which appear to exhibit similar behaviour.
Résumé

Ils suggèrent l'idée que, au-dessus de la température de transition seulement, la germination des fissures est plus facile que leur propagation et que la rupture fragile se produit pour la contrainte entraînant la germination des fissures. Ils pensent que, à la température de transition, les contraintes correspondant à la limite élastique, à la germination des fissures et à leur propagation sont égales entre elles. En supposant qu'il existe des relations phénoménologiques simples respectivement entre la contrainte de limite élastique et la température, la vitesse de déformation et la taille des grains, les auteurs présentent une étude de l'influence de la vitesse de déformation, de l'irradiation aux neutrons et de la taille des grains sur la température de transition ductile-fragile. Cette étude doit être applicable,aux autres métaux de transition c.c. du groupe VIa, chrome et tungstène, qui semblent avoir un comportement analogue.
